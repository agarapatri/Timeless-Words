Overview

The Bhagavad Gita, often referred to as the Gita, is a 700-verse Hindu scripture that is part of the epic Mahabharata. Set in a narrative framework of a dialogue between Prince Arjuna and his guide and charioteer Krishna, the Gita addresses the moral and philosophical dilemmas faced by Arjuna on the battlefield of Kurukshetra.

This timeless text explores fundamental questions about duty (dharma), righteousness, and the nature of reality. Through Krishna's teachings, the Gita presents multiple paths to spiritual realization, including the paths of knowledge (jnana yoga), devotion (bhakti yoga), and righteous action (karma yoga).

The Bhagavad Gita has been influential in the development of Hindu philosophy and has been studied and revered by scholars, philosophers, and spiritual seekers across cultures and centuries.

Historical Context

Composition Period

Estimated between 5th century BCE to 2nd century CE, with scholarly consensus around 400 BCE to 400 CE.

Literary Context

Forms chapters 25-42 of the Bhishma Parva in the Mahabharata, the world's longest epic poem.

Philosophical Schools

Synthesizes various Hindu philosophical traditions including Samkhya, Yoga, and Vedanta.

Cultural Impact

Influenced leaders like Mahatma Gandhi and has been translated into over 75 languages worldwide.

Key Themes

Dharma & Duty

Exploration of righteous duty and moral obligations in the face of difficult circumstances.

Paths of Yoga

Three primary paths to spiritual realization: knowledge, devotion, and selfless action.

Divine Nature

Krishna's revelation of his cosmic form and teachings on the divine presence in all beings.

Liberation

The ultimate goal of moksha (liberation) and freedom from the cycle of birth and death.
